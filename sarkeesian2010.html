SARKEESIAN,

Heroic women in science fiction and fantasy television shows have done much to represent strong, successful women in leadership positions. However, these female roles that are viewed as strong and empowered embody many masculine identified traits, maintaining a patriarchal division of gender roles (SARKEESIAN, 2010, p. ii)

SARKEESIAN,

This project began as I was watching TV and found myself identifying with and rooting for the strong female heroes. As I looked critically at their roles, I noticed that many of them were replicating the traditional male hero archetype and ‘masculine’ defined values (SARKEESIAN, 2010, p. iii)

SARKEESIAN,

The women that are identified as strong and tough, nearly always possess physical strength, rarely ask for help, and hardly ever show emotion unless forced. Strength remains a central attribute to female characters, and is seemingly highly valued both by society and within fandoms (SARKEESIAN, 2010, p. 07)

SARKEESIAN,

While there are instances of female ‘braniacs' and scientists, and of sensitive women who help devise plans, or are good wives, daughters, mothers or teachers, these traits are often reserved for supporting characters, not starring roles. (SARKEESIAN, 2010, p. 07)

SARKEESIAN,

I especially gravitated towards science fiction and fantasy programs because as I was attempting to imagine a future economic and social system that is rooted in social justice values, I anticipated that science fiction was a place that would portray futuristic societies with different values. (SARKEESIAN, 2010, p. 08)

SARKEESIAN,

I also began to notice that I identified with and enjoyed watching the women who I was viewing. They have many commonalities: they were strong, in charge, capable, confident and intelligent. (SARKEESIAN, 2010, p. 08)
